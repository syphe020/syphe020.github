Chapter I

"Why is she rotting in the dungeons?"

Preston's boots thundered against the tiles as he stormed behind his brother, rage coursing through his veins like molten iron. The massive doors to Damien's private quarters groaned open, and Preston's vision blurred red at the edges. His brother—the King of Redwin—had committed an act so vile it made his stomach churn with revulsion.

Damien's mate. An omega. Barren. And he threw her in the dungeons and destroyed her bond in return.

"Preston, I've already explained this travesty," Damien snarled, his voice dripping with venom as he swept into his opulent chambers. "We were deceived. Those bastards sent us damaged goods—a worthless omega who cannot produce an heir. There can be no peace treaty built on lies and broken promises."

Preston's fingers raked through his hair, nails digging into his scalp. "So your grand solution is execution? To butcher the omega and parade her corpse as a warning?" His voice cracked like a whip. "You'll drag us all back into that blood-soaked nightmare, Damien. A war that has already devoured too many lives."

Damien's laugh was cold as winter steel as he sank into his marble bath, water rippling around his form. "Then we'll finish what we started. Burn their cities to ash, salt their fields, and claim their lands as our own." His eyes glittered with cruel satisfaction. "I will not be remembered as a weak king."

Every muscle in Preston's body coiled tight. His soldiers—his people—finally tasting freedom after twelve years of hell, and this madman wanted to throw them back into the grinder.

"No."

The word exploded from his chest. "I'll claim the omega. Keep the treaty intact, and I'll take her as mine." He stalked closer, like a predator closing on prey. "You're a king, brother. Omegas would crawl through broken glass for a chance at your bed. Choose another." His voice booms against the opulent tiles, reverberating with anger;

"Give me the omega."

Damien's face twisted in disgust. "I won't watch my brother—my general—chain himself to used goods. A barren, broken thing with scars and severed bonds."

"I won't watch my soldiers bleed for your pride, Damien."

The temperature in the room plummeted. Damien's eyes flashed dangerous gold. "Those soldiers serve their king, not you, Preston. Remember your place."

Preston stepped forward, alpha power radiating from every pore. Brother or not, king or not—this ended here. "Those men follow me into hell because I follow you. Their loyalty to the crown runs through my veins." Another step. "Give them peace. Give me the omega."

For a heartbeat, the brothers stared each other down—two alphas on the knife's edge of violence. Then Damien's expression cracked, something almost resembling fondness bleeding through.

"Lucky for you, blood runs thicker than pride." He waved a dismissive hand. "Take her, then. But we discuss terms tomorrow." As Preston turned to leave, Damien's voice followed him like a ghost. "Welcome home, brother."

The hallway stretched before him like a battlefield. Preston's first command rang through the corridors: "Escort me to the omega in the dungeons. Now."

He had a peace treaty to honor—and an omega to claim.

—

Sat on the rotting dungeon floors, Maev stills at the distant sound of footsteps. Her back is straight and her posture poised—a lifetime of lessons had wrought this body. Her face is angled towards one of the few torchlights still burning, her eyelids closed, as if she's imagining the torch to be the sun, yearning for the warmth on her sun-kissed skin.

The gate of the outer dungeons opened, the crunch of boots against the putrid floor echoed, but she did not move. She can sense people approaching her cell, yet not a single muscle of hers twitched.

Eyes still closed, she held but a single question. "Is it time for my execution?"

Preston’s breath caught in his throat as he took in the sight of her—this omega, once a royal offering, now slumped in filth but still holding herself with impossible dignity. The dungeon stench clung to the air, thick with rot and despair, but she sat like a queen on a throne of shadows, face tilted toward the flickering torchlight as if it were the sun itself.

For the first time in years, something in his chest ached.

He crouched in front of the bars, close enough to scent the remnants of her ruined bond—bitter and frayed, like a severed thread still clinging to the needle. His alpha instincts coiled tight, a low, protective growl rumbling in his chest before he could stop it.

"No," he said, voice rough with too many unsaid things. "You’re not dying today."

Her lashes fluttered, but she still did not open her eyes. "Then what use does the King have for a barren omega?"

Preston’s jaw flexed. "None," he admitted. "But I do."

That finally made her turn. Dark eyes, sharp and unflinching despite everything, locked onto his. He saw the defiance there—and beneath it, the exhaustion. The resignation. The kind that came from knowing your own body had betrayed you in the worst way.

She exhaled, a bitter laugh on her tongue. "So," she mused, "the great General Preston Alexander needs a useless omega to warm his bed now?"

His fingers curled around the bars. "I need," he ground out, "to keep my soldiers from marching back into war. And for that, I need you."

A beat. Then, quieter: "Alive."

Her gaze flickered, something unreadable passing through it. "And if I refuse?"

Preston didn’t hesitate. "Then you’ll die here."

A slow, almost amused smile curled her lips. "So my choices are death—or you?"

He tilted his head, studying her—this omega who should have been broken by now but wasn’t. "Surely I am the better option," he muttered.

She let out a soft, humorless sound. Then, with a grace that shouldn’t have been possible in chains, she rose to her feet. "And if your soldiers do not march, my country will not be burnt to ash."

Preston exhaled—a slow, measured breath—as he watched her rise. Even in the dim torchlight, even with the filth of the dungeon clinging to her skin, she moved like royalty. Like power. And that sharp, calculating look in her eyes—this omega was no helpless creature.

Maev settles her gaze to the General, "Yes. You are a far better option that I never thought possible."

"Good," he said simply, straightening to his full height. Then, to the guards behind him: "Keys."

A moment later, the cell door groaned open, the rusted hinges screaming in protest. Preston stepped inside, close enough now that her scent—warm spice and something floral, buried beneath the stench of captivity—hit him like a physical blow.

He reached out, calloused fingers brushing the ruined mating mark on her neck. A quiet fury burned in his chest at the sight of it—the broken bond, the jagged scar left behind. His alpha instincts snarled at the disrespect.

"You won’t be called barren again," he said, voice low. A promise. A threat.

To who? Even he wasn’t sure.

Maev balks at his words, "Yet I am."

"An insignificant matter when you are the key to peace," he pressed through clenched teeth, feeling combative over her bantering.

Then, because he was still a soldier at heart, he turned practical. "You’ll be taken to my quarters. Bathed. Fed. A healer will see to you." He paused, considering. "Unless you’d prefer to stay in the filth?"

Her lips curved, just slightly. "How generous of you, General."

He grunted. "Don’t get used to it." Then, softer, as he guided her out of the cell: "We’ll discuss terms later."

She tilted her head, studying him with those sharp, knowing eyes. "And what terms would those be?"

Preston’s mouth twitched. "The kind where you don’t end up back in a cell."

A beat. Then, a rare flicker of amusement in her gaze. "I think I can work with that."

He nodded once, gruff. Good.

Preston motioned to the opened cell gate, chivalrous in the presence of a woman. Maev moved like smoke, slipping past him without contact, but the moment she was free of the cell, her knees buckled—fatigue and malnourishment finally winning.

Preston caught her before she hit the ground, his arm banding around her waist as he hauled her upright against his side. She was lighter than she should be.

"Easy," he muttered, more to himself than her. His alpha instincts flared, furious at the state of her—the dried blood at her nape where Damien had torn the bond, the scent of distress clinging to her skin.

She stiffened, then sagged, as if the fight had drained out of her the moment she was touched. "I won't thank you," she said quietly.

Preston tightened his grip, shifting her weight more securely against him. "Didn't ask you to." His voice was rough, but the arm around her waist held steady—unyielding as stone.

A guard stepped forward, hand outstretched as if to take her from him.

Preston's growl was instantaneous, a deep warning. The guard froze, eyes wide, then quickly backed off.

The thought came unbidden, primal and possessive. He shoved it down—now wasn't the time.

"General—" she started, but he cut her off.

"You walk out of here on your own feet, or I carry you." A pause. "Choose."

She exhaled, a sharp, irritated sound. Then, with visible effort, she pushed upright, her spine straightening despite the tremble in her legs. "I walk."

Preston didn't remove his hand from her back. Just in case.

"Good."

And together—her pride and his stubbornness—they stepped out of the dungeon and into the torchlit halls of the palace.

Chapter II

The walk to his quarters was too long, and Maev's breaths is too shallow. Every faltering step made his alpha rage simmer hotter. By the time he kicked open his chamber door, his teeth ached with the need to fix this.

"Bath's ready, sir," a timid servant announced from the corner before leaving. Preston had sent word ahead—efficient as always.

He set Maev down on the edge of the bed, his hands lingering a beat too long to ensure she wouldn't topple. "Strip. Wash. Eat." He jerked his chin toward the steaming copper tub in the adjoined washroom. "You look like death."

She arched a brow. "And here I thought you wanted me alive."

A muscle jumped in his cheek. He turned to leave, but her voice hooked him like a blade between the ribs—

"You are really doing this?" Soft. Uncertain. The first crack in her armor.

Preston stopped mid-step, his back still to her. The fire crackled in the hearth, casting long shadows across the stone floor. For a moment, the only sound was the faint slosh of water in the tub, the shift of fabric as she sat there—waiting.

He turned his head just enough to see her from the corner of his eye.

"You think I dragged you out of that dungeon for fun?" His voice was low, edged with something raw.

Her fingers tightened in the blankets. "I think," she said carefully, "that you are a man who has spent his life following orders. And I am concerned if— when the orders will change."

She studied him, dark eyes searching. "If your brother changes his mind? If he decides I'm more trouble than I'm worth?"

Preston turned fully then, crossing his arms. "Then he'll have to go through me."

The words hung between them, heavy with unspoken meaning.

She exhaled, shoulders relaxing slightly. Then, with deliberate slowness, she reaches for the laces of her ruined dress.

Preston's pulse kicked—but he didn’t move.

She paused, arching a brow. "You planning to watch, General?"

He held her gaze for a beat longer than necessary. Then, with a grunt, he turned on his heel and strode for the door.

"I would not mind," she suddenly calls out, as if it is a challenge. "Though I must warn you, the state of my body is not for the weak of heart."

Her words were not meant to seduce him, it was an invitation to witness what the King —his brother— had done to her.

Preston's hand froze on the door handle, fingers digging into the worn metal. Every instinct in him howled to turn around, to see the damage for himself, to let the sight of her suffering fuel the fire already burning in his chest.

She was offering proof.

A testimony of cruelty.

He exhaled sharply through his nose and turned, his gaze sweeping over her with deliberate detachment.

"Show me."

Not a request. Not even a demand. Just a soldier assessing the battlefield.

Maev held his stare as she tugged the laces loose, her movements slow but unwavering. The fabric slid from her shoulders like a whispered confession, pooling at her waist before she let it fall completely.

Preston's jaw locked.

Bruises. Old and new, mottling her ribs, scarring her back, her hips, the tops of her thighs. Scars—some clean and thin, others ragged and poorly healed—crossing her skin like a map of suffering. The worst of them circled her wrists, the marks of shackles worn too long and too tight.

But it was the jagged ruin at the base of her nape that made his vision tint red.

Damien hadn't just broken their bond.

He'd ravaged it.

Maev tilted her chin up, her dark eyes daring him to look away. "Still want to stake your claim on damaged goods, General?"

Preston crossed the room in three strides.

His hand closed around her wrist—gently, for once—thumb brushing over the scarred skin there.

"These don't make you damaged," he growled. "They make you alive."

Her breath hitched.

He released her just as abruptly, stepping back before his alpha instincts could override his common sense. "Now get in the damn bath before I throw you in myself."

Maev's lips curved, just slightly.

And for the first time since he'd pulled her from that cell—it almost looked like a real smile.

"...Thank you." She whispered quietly without meeting his golden eyes. There is truth in his words. She is alive.

As per his order, Maev stood, now fully naked, as she carefully makes her way to the steaming copper tub. With every step, she seemed to wince. The pain did not just came from the marks on her skin.

It will hurt. The water will hurt some of her open wounds. She hesitates for a moment.

Preston saw the hesitation. Saw the tension in her shoulders as she stared down at the steaming water. He knew exactly what she was thinking—hot water on broken skin burned.

Without a word, he crossed to the side table where the servants had left a pitcher of cool water. He grabbed it, then stepped up beside the tub.

"Wait," he grunted.

Then he poured the cool water into the bath, tempering the heat until it was warm rather than scalding. Steam still curled from the surface, but it wouldn’t sear her wounds.

He set the pitcher down harder than necessary, his jaw tight.

"Try now."

Maev blinked at the water, then at him. He refused to meet her eyes, staring instead at the far wall like it held the answers to the damn war.

She dipped a hand in, testing. Then exhaled—just a little—as the warmth soothed rather than punished.

He didn’t watch as she stepped in. But he didn’t leave, either.

Some things were worse than pain.

Like being alone with it.

She sank into the water with a quiet sigh, her body finally relaxing for what was likely the first time in months. Something in his chest tightened.

"...The temperature is well."

Preston hadn't moved from his spot by the wall, arms crossed, gaze averted - but alert to every sound of water shifting, every quiet breath she took.

Maev scooped some water in her palms and gently wipes her face, washing off the dirt and grime from her ears, her hair, her feet. She cleans herself methodically and carefully, being extremely cautious with her wounds.

When she rose from the bath, he finally turned his head, just enough to confirm she was steady before looking away again. He hands her a drying cloth while his eyes are elsewhere.

A fresh set of clothes had been laid out on the bed - simple but well-made, soft linen rather than roughspun. Preston jerked his chin toward them. "Put those on. Then eat."

The small table near the hearth held a tray of food—broth, bread, fruit. Nothing too heavy for a stomach that had likely gone empty too long.

He moved to the window, giving her privacy to dress, staring out at the training yards below where soldiers still drilled even as evening fell. His fingers tapped restlessly against the stone sill.

"I am decently dressed, General." Maev informed him, now dressed in the smooth linens that he provided. The fabric is soft against her skin and wounds, not at all hurtful.

Preston then motioned to the food on the small table near the hearth, "Eat."

She eyed the food, then him. "You are not joining me?"

His stomach chose that moment to growl loudly, betraying him. He hadn't eaten since before the dungeon. Maev's lips twitched.

Preston scowled. "Fine." He dragged the second chair out with his boot and sat, tearing off a chunk of bread with more force than necessary. "But chew properly. I won't have you choking after all this."

The corner of her mouth lifted as she sat across from him, the drying cloth now wrapped around her damp hair. "How very considerate of you, General."

He grunted, shoveling broth into his mouth to avoid responding. The silence stretched, comfortable in a way it shouldn't have been between near-strangers. The fire crackled. Somewhere outside, a sentry called the hour.

Maev broke it first, voice quiet but steady. "When will you claim me?"

Preston's spoon stilled halfway to his mouth. The broth dripped back into the bowl, unheeded.

His gaze lifted to hers, the firelight catching the hardened edge of his stare.

"Tomorrow." No hesitation. No sugarcoating. "Before the court. Before my brother can change his mind."

He set the spoon down with deliberate calm. "You'll be presented as mine. Officially. Publicly."

A muscle ticked in his jaw. "It won't be gentle."

Bonding never was. Especially not when it had to be a show.

Maev didn't flinch. Just studied him with those dark, knowing eyes. "I don't need gentle."

Preston's fingers twitched toward his dagger—his tell, when instincts warred with reason. "You'll need to be strong enough to take my bite." His voice dropped, gravel-rough. "Can you?"

She didn't blink. "Will it keep your soldiers from dying?"

"Yes."

"Then I can."

Simple. Unshakable.

Preston exhaled through his nose and picked his spoon back up. "Eat," he muttered. "You'll need your strength."

Maev kept her eyes curiously to the general as she chews on a piece of fruit. She knows his rank, his relationship with the King, his loyalty and compassion to his people...

But who is General Preston as a person?

"Then if you plan to claim me tomorrow, I am assuming a healer will be called before then?"

His gaze darts straight to her neck, at the clawed bruise around what was supposed to be the king's bond.

Preston's fingers tightened around his spoon, knuckles whitening. The ruined bond mark stood out livid against her skin—a grotesque mockery of what should have been sacred. His alpha instincts rumbled at the sight, fury and protectiveness warring in his chest.

"Yes," he bit out. The word came out sharper than intended, the weight of his anger slipping through. He forced his grip to ease, exhaling through his nose. "A healer will see to you tonight. And again tomorrow before..."

He trailed off, jaw working.

Before he had to scar her all over again.

Maev tilted her head, watching him with that unnerving perceptiveness. "Does it bother you?"

Preston's gaze snapped back to hers.

She gestured to her neck, to the mangled remnants of Damien's claim. "This. The idea of putting another mark over it."

A loaded question. A dangerous one.

Preston set his spoon down with deliberate care. "What bothers me," he said, low and measured, "is that my brother treated an omega like carrion instead of a mate." His lip curled slightly. "A bond should never be made—or broken—in cruelty."

Maev studied him for a long moment. Then, quietly: "You are not like him."

It wasn't a question.

Preston scoffed, pushing back from the table. "Don't mistake necessity for virtue, omega. I'm claiming you to stop a war. Not out of some noble fucking gesture."

Maev's lips quirked. "And yet, you cooled my bathwater."

Preston froze.

Damn her.

He removed himself from the table, his plate already cleared. Then he turned on his heel and stalked toward the door. "I'll fetch the healer," he growled over his shoulder. "Eat."

The door slammed closed.

Left alone on his private quarters, Maev continues to eat while allowing her eyes to gaze around the room. At the small touches that might hint of his personality.

The general's quarter is almost bare, filled with only practical necessities, nothing to indicate his hobbies or interests. She frowns as she chews, 'What a duty-bound man. Does he not find joy in anything?'

Then her eyes caught sight of a line of smooth rocks, lined on his closed windowsill. It looked to be river rocks, its surface smoothed by water. 'Huh... Does the general perhaps prefer collecting rocks?'

As she thought that to herself, the front door unlocks.

Preston's heavy boots scuffed against the stone floor as he entered, the healer—a no-nonsense beta woman with silver streaking her dark braid—close behind. His gaze immediately snapped to the tray of food, assessing how much Maev had eaten.

The healer, Mistress Hesta, didn’t waste time with pleasantries. "Let’s see the damage then," she said briskly, setting her satchel down on the bed.

Maev stood, obedient but not meek, letting the woman examine the ruined bond mark. Hesta's lips thinned as she gestured to the torn flesh. "This was done with malice," she muttered. "No attempt to clean or stitch it."

Preston’s exhale was sharp through his nose, his hand flexing at his side like he wanted to wrap it around someone’s throat. Damien’s, most likely.

"I’ll need to treat the infection first," Hesta continued, rummaging through her bag. "Then something to numb the area before tomorrow." She shot Preston a pointed look. "Especially before tomorrow."

He gave a stiff nod, then abruptly turned toward the door. "I’ll be outside, Mistress Hesta."

Maev arched a brow. "Running away again, General?"

Preston didn’t turn around, but his shoulders tensed—a tell she was learning quickly. "Guarding the door. You're safe here," he corrected, voice gruff. "Unless you’d prefer an audience for your examination?"

Mistress Hesta rolled her eyes, already uncorking a vial of pungent salve. "Out, Alexander. My patient doesn’t need your growling making her flinch."

Preston left without another word, shutting the door harder than necessary behind him.

Maev could not help herself but chuckle at the irritation he displayed.

Hesta snorted. "That one’s got the temper of a provoked boar." She dabbed salve onto Maev’s wounds, her touch firm but not unkind. "But he’s right about one thing—you are safe here."

Maev tilted her head. "You sound certain."

Hesta's hands didn't pause as she smoothed a poultice over the worst of Maev's bruises. "Because I've been patching up the General's soldiers for twenty years," she said matter-of-factly. "And in all that time, I've never seen him break a promise—especially not one involving someone under his protection."

She pressed a clean bandage to Maev's shoulder with practiced efficiency. "That man would rather cut off his own sword arm than let harm come to you now."

Maev exhaled slowly as the numbing herbs began to dull the ache of her wounds. "Even though I am just a political tool to him?"

Hesta gave her a long, knowing look. "Child, if that were true, he wouldn't have nearly kicked down my door demanding I come immediately." She snorted. "And he certainly wouldn't have those ridiculous rocks lining his windowsill."

Maev blinked. "You know about those?"

The older woman rolled her eyes. "Every time his troops march near the White Rapids, half the company comes back with their pockets full of smooth stones for him. Says he likes the weight of them when he's strategizing." She tied off the last bandage with a sharp tug. "That's not the habit of a man who sees people as tools."

Outside the door, a boot scuffed loudly against stone—as if the General had been leaning against it a little too closely.

Maev bit back a smile as the healer packed her supplies, the warmth in her chest having nothing to do with the fire in the hearth. Hesta then gave her some nightly ointments, informing her of how to apply them.

"Thank you, Mistress Hesta." Maev's voice was earnest as she bows her head at the old woman, appreciative that the scars and bruises on her body are being cared for by her healing touch.

Hesta left the quarters, having a short talk with Preston outside, before the general returns to the warmth of his abode.

Maev is at his bed, the majority of her body either patched up or wrapped in bandages. She decided to inform Preston of what the healer informed her, "I am tasked to sleep not on my back."

Preston recalls the criss-crossed whip marks on her back.

"She had told me to apply this gel to my back before bed, and to reapply it every six bells, but... I do not think she meant for me to put it on myself." Too prideful to ask for help directly, Maev twisted her words to sound like an order from the healer.

Preston exhaled sharply through his nose—that same irritated sound he always made when someone tried to maneuver him into something. But his hands were already moving, snatching the small jar of medicinal gel from the bedside table with more force than necessary.

"Turn," he ordered, voice gruff.

She raised a brow, "But I am not ready for bed."

"Turn."

Maev obeyed with a sigh, shifting carefully onto her stomach on the bed. The firelight flickered over the lattice of scars, the fresh bandages stark against her sun-kissed skin.

Preston's jaw tightened as he knelt beside the bed. His fingers—battle-calloused, sword-worn—dipped into the jar with surprising care, scooping out a dollop of the pale green salve.

"This'll be cold," he warned.

Then his touch landed between her shoulder blades, spreading the gel in slow, methodical circles. His palm was rough, but his pressure was exact—firm enough to work the medicine in, gentle enough not to press too hard on the wounds beneath.

Maev stiffened at first, then gradually relaxed as the numbing herbs took effect.

Preston worked in silence, his breathing steady, his focus absolute—as if tending her wounds was just another battlefield duty. But when his thumb brushed a particularly jagged scar near her ribs, he paused.

"Who did this?" The question was quiet. Deadly.

Maev turned her head just enough to peer at him over her shoulder. "Does it matter?"

Preston's fingers stilled. "Yes."

A beat. Then, softer: "Because tomorrow, you'll bear my mark. And I don't share what's mine with ghosts."

Maev held his gaze for a long moment before answering. "The King's interrogators. When they thought I was withholding information about my homeland."

Preston's expression darkened. But all he said was, "Hn."

Then he went back to his task, his touch lingering just a second longer than necessary on each scar—as if he could wipe them away through sheer stubbornness alone.

When he finished, he recapped the jar with a decisive snap. "Sleep," he ordered, pushing to his feet. "I'll wake you for the next application."

Maev studied him—this gruff, duty-bound man who collected river rocks and growled at healers and touched her like she was something fragile. It made her curious.

Maev glanced over her shoulder, that familiar challenging glint in her eye. "Where will you be sleeping, General?"

Preston pointed to the armor stand in the corner where his greatcoat hung. "Right there. On the floor."

Her nose wrinkled. "That's ridiculous."

"And you're injured," he shot back. "The bed's yours tonight."

For a long moment, they stared each other down—two stubborn souls caught between pride and practicality. Then Maev surprised them both by scooting over, leaving just enough space on the large bed for a broad-shouldered alpha.

"The ceremony tomorrow will not go well if you're stiff from the floor," she said primly, as if discussing battle tactics rather than sleeping arrangements.

Preston's jaw worked like he was chewing through a dozen retorts before settling on a grumbled, "Fine."

He stripped off his weapons belt with military efficiency, hanging it within arm's reach on the bedpost. The greatcoat stayed on—too many scars of his own hidden beneath it—but his boots hit the floor with twin thuds.

The bed dipped under his weight as he stretched out rigidly on his back, just a foot of space between them. The dimming embers of the firelight caught the tension in his shoulders, the way his fingers flexed against his thighs like he was restraining himself from reaching for a sword.

Maev turned onto her uninjured side, studying his profile—the stubborn set of his jaw, the shadows beneath his eyes that spoke of too many nights standing watch, the gold hair grown wild and unkempt from months of journey. General Preston Alexander, she begrudgingly admits to herself, is rather handsome.

"Sleep," he ordered, voice gone rough. "That's an order."

Maev closes her eyes. For the first time since arriving in this cursed kingdom, she obeyed without hesitation.

Chapter III

In the dead of night, a familiar nightmare returns in her dreams. Of her body being beaten, used, mutilated. Of herself bracing for impact, for abuse, for pain.

Maev's body jolted in the waking world, sweating. Her eyes are shut tight, still trapped in her nightmare, yet she mutters out broken apologies in her sleep.

"...For... forgive me... forgive me......"

Preston was awake before the first whimper even left her lips—soldier’s instincts wired deep. His hand was already on her shoulder when she jolted, gripping just tight enough to ground without restraining.

"Maev." His voice was a low rumble against the dark—firm, but not sharp.

She shuddered, still caught in the nightmare’s claws, her body coiled tight with phantom blows.

Preston shifted without hesitation, rolling onto his side fully to face her. He didn’t pull her close. Didn’t smother. Just pressed his forehead to hers—hard—an alpha’s instinctive response to an omega in distress. His scent—iron and cedar and the salt of old battlefields—wrapped around her like armor.

"Breathe," he ordered, voice gravel-rough with sleep.

Her gasp was ragged, but her lungs obeyed before her mind caught up. Air rushed in, then out—stealing the nightmare’s grip. Her lashes fluttered open, meeting his in the dim moonlight.

Preston didn’t look away.

No pity in his gaze.

No softness, either.

Just certainty.

"You're here," he said, like it was law. Like it was the only truth that mattered. "With me."

Maev exhaled, long and slow.

His thumb brushed her collarbone—once, twice—a silent I’m here against her skin.

Then he rolled back, arm outstretched. An open invitation.

No words.

No shame.

Just the quiet understanding of soldiers who knew the weight of bad dreams.

Maev curled into him.

This time, the night stayed silent.

—

Sun breaks, and Maev awoke to Preston stretching, seated on the bed's edge. He seems ready to tackle the morning, even though the sun is not fully out yet.

"Going somewhere, General?" Her voice is still thick with sleep, her wounds making her body sore as she carefully sits up.

"Training."

"This early?" Maev glances to the window, now slightly opened to let the cool air in.

"Every dawn. Discipline is nurtured."

She hums, eyes following him as he slid in his boots.

Then, with a quieter voice, Maev admits. "Last night... I may have inconvenienced you. I apologize, General."

Preston paused mid-motion as he strapped on his bracers, the leather creaking in the quiet room. He didn't turn around, but his shoulders tensed slightly beneath his linen undershirt.

For her.

For all of them.

Outside, the winds of change howled.

And Redwin held its breath.

Chapter VIII

Not only a moon later.

Redwin had its true ruler at last.

The future, just beginning.
